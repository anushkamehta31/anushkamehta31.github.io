Introduction¶

The World Happiness Report is an annual report by the UN that measures the state of worldwide bliss. It is released on March 20th annually, the International Day of Happiness. The report is a landmark survey that ranks 156 nations by how happy their citizens perceive themselves to be. The report examines how factors such as governments, organizations and respectful society affects levels of overall happiness. By estimating a nation's level of well being, we can begin to determine levels of advancement. The goal of the report is to “give more importance to happiness and well-being in determining how to achieve and measure social and economic development.” By understanding how different environments such as social, environmental and natural living conditions affect prosperity, the foundation hopes to examine the evolution of world bliss. Moreover, it takes into account extensively how technologies, social norms, conflicts and government policies that have driven changes in prosperity over the years. If readers are interested in reading the latest World Happiness Report it can be found here: https://worldhappiness.report/ed/2020/

What actually constitutes a happiness score?¶

Since happiness is not generally considered a quantifiable variable, you might be wondering how exactly the UN devises such a score. The score is actually composed from the Gallup World Poll datasets and used seven factors in its calculation. For each factor, (or variable), individuals rank their scores from 0 to 10. The scores are then normalized as well as compared against a dystopia, (a hypothetical nation with the lowest score in each category).

We will attempt to determine which factors are most significant in determining the happiness score for a particular nation by examining the data sets of the World Happiness Report over the course of six years and then make predictions about data throughout the years. After we figure our which of the seven variables are most influential in determining comparative rank, we will use linear regression models to attempt to make predict the actual scores in 156 nations. Our goal will be to go through the steps of data analysis including data collection, data tidying, data analysis and hypothesis testing.

Our columns include Happiness Rank, Happiness Score, GDP, Family and Social Status, Life Expectancy, Freedom, Government Corruption, Generosity and Dystopia Residual.

Let us define our variables:

Happiness Rank pertains the nations rank comparative to other nations being assessed.

Happiness Score is calculated by adding the normalized rankings of the nations scores in each category.

Economy (GDP per Capita) is where individuals rate their happiness in life based on their earnings.

Family is where individuals rank the quality of their family lives.

Health (Life Expectancy) is determined by how available health care is in the nation and life expectancy.

Freedom pertains to how much independence a person is able to enjoy in his daily life.

Trust (Government Corruption) is ranked by how trusting the individual is of his or her government not to be corrupt.

Generosity involves how willing the individual's nation is in aiding worldly affairs such as peace keeping.

Dystopia Residual is calculated by adding the nation's residual with the score of a hypothetical nation with lowest scores.

Multiple linear regression involves observing the effects of multiple indpendent variables on a dependent variable. We are attempting to measure the extent to which there exists a linear relationship between variables.
